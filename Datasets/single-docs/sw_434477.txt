William F. Buckley, Jr.

William Frank Buckley, Jr. (November 24, 1925 – February 27, 2008) was an American conservative author and commentator. He founded the political magazine "National Review" in 1955, which had a major impact in stimulating the conservative movement. He endorsed Barry M. Goldwater and Ronald Reagan. 

He hosted 1,429 episodes of the television show "Firing Line" from 1966 until 1999, where he became known for his transatlantic accent and wide vocabulary. He also wrote a nationally syndicated newspaper column, and wrote numerous spy novels.

Buckley was born November 24, 1925, in New York City. He was of Irish descent. He studied at Beaumont College. In 1950, Buckley married Patricia Buckley. They had a son, Christopher.

Buckley died at his home in Stamford, Connecticut, on February 27, 2008. Initially, it was reported that he was found dead at his desk in his study, a converted garage. At the time of his death, he had been suffering from emphysema and diabetes. His cause of death was soon ruled as emphysema.
